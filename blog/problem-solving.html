Problem Solving

1. when was I blocked on a simple problem
Anytime I get stuck on a problem while programming, for any amount of time even if its 5 minutes, I take a step back from the problem and try to think about what the steps need to be. A couple years ago I got stuck while trying to apply rules to chess pieces in java. The issue was that I didnt really have a gauge at the time on how they behaved other than manually trying to move them after running the program so when unexpected outcomes occurred I was confused as to why the behavior was seemingly so far off of what I had been trying to create. When trying to figure out the knights movement I took a step away from my IDE and wrote down what needed to happen on paper. On my chess grid a knight needed to be able to move in all directions 3 cells in one direction and then 1 in the other direction to create the L shape. I drew a grid on paper to represent my board and wrote all of the possible movements down, noting that they were +3x +1y, -3x -1y and so on. . I felt pretty useless trying to figure it out in my head until I wrote stuff down so I wasnt just trying to figure out and remember combinations in my head. I learned that taking breaks and planning properly make a huge difference in my workflow and make problems much easier to understand, especially at a point where they feel like a wall.

2. a time when I elegantly solved a problem
I feel although quite a few of my solutions to Javascript kata were somewhat elegant, at least in my mind they were neatly presented and worked, with the exception of my capitalize method where I wanted to test out some methods I had been learning about. Pseudocoding helped me get a grasp on the steps I needed to take to solve the problems and reading the test results was a huge help in understanding what needed to be done to get the result I was looking for, whether that be reading what is currently being outputted or seeing the formatting of the string I was returning rather than the expected result. I felt pretty good throughout the whole process as I was using similar tools to those I have used in the past, quite a few of javascripts methods such as map/forEach/reduce are very similar to streams in java which I have a decent amount of experience with. I learned a lot that I CAN do in javascript which I was unaware of to some degree prior to testing things out and having a play around with new methods.

3. reflection on how confident I feel using the following problem solving techniques/processes
Pseudocode - very confident, I generally pseudocode before starting on anything

Trying something - very confident, most of the time I play around with different ideas to solve a problem before settling on something

Rubber Ducky Method - quite confident, I do this sometimes but its not generally my first idea to resolve bugs, I will read through my code line by line and ask myself what it should be resulting in.

Reading error messages - very confident, test results and error messages are bread and butter for me in understanding what is going wrong with a problem

console.logging - somewhat confident, I used to do this a lot more when I worked in java and just .println whenever I wanted to see what something was returning at any point in a method, less so now but I still use this technique from time to time

Googling - very confident, I got very good at googling during my time at uni, specifically trying to find problems in the same wheelhouse as what I was working with or issues while using the same method

Asking your peers for help - not so confident, I dont generally like asking for help unless I feel like Im absolutely stuck, which is not a good thing and I have been trying to get better at asking for help

Asking coaches for help - somewhat confident, In the same vein as asking my peers for help although I have been better at asking for help in the public forum of the tech questions channel in discord

Improving your process with reflection - very confident, Prior to attending Dev Academy I did this passively to a much lower degree with regards to programming but in other walks of life I learn the most from reflection
